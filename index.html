    Who is PayPal?

    You have heard of PayPal, right? PayPal is one of the biggest payment players in the world. By using their service, you can transfer money to your friends or pay for things you bought online. Their goal is to give you the feeling of security when transferring money to a stranger from some random online shop. Online shops that provide the possibility to pay with PayPal seem more trustworthy - after all, it is a big company with millions of users that claims to ensure safe transactions. And if many people use it, then it must be good, right?

    Using PayPal is free for us as users, so how come it makes billions as a company? Well, as a general rule, you can remember: If you don’t pay with money for a service, then you most likely pay with your data. And that’s also the case with PayPal. Yes, PayPal needs some information from you to process a payment. But what seems suspicious is that PayPal collects much more information than what is essential to process a simple online payment. Storing that much data on servers costs money. Since they have over 400 million users, this would be extremely expensive. So, there must be a catch!

    The data you create is like an internet footprint to collect information about your person and create a profile. This profile in turn is used to predict your future behaviors on the internet. Why? Because someone profits from it. How do they profit? They sell their predictions about your possible behavior to other companies, which then make sure you see an advertisement on instagram about those new headphones you were looking at.

    How does PayPal get all of our very personal data?

    Sometimes, your choices are not really yours, and you don't always make a decision on your own. Companies like PayPal use tricks to influence you:
    Imagine that in your school cafeteria, apples and water could be on the display, while chips and coke could be placed under the counter. Everyone could still make the choice for themselves, but they would be looking at healthy food while waiting, and might even not be fully aware of the other options. It would just be easier to get something that is easily reachable and visible - thus nudging people to buy healthy food instead of the unhealthy one. (Although usually, chips and coke are on display and not the healthy stuff, watch out!)

    Although you are ultimately free to decide, what you agreed to is most probably the result of companies’ efforts and psychological tricks. People’s behavior is predictable!

    But can’t I just refuse to provide them with all that personal data?

    Whenever you make an online payment, download an app or use any kind of service, you are asked to agree to a document called ‘Terms & Conditions’ or ‘User Agreement’. For online services and websites, you often have to tick a small box that you agree to the document before you can continue and use the service. Let’s be honest, have you ever actually read that document? Don’t be ashamed if you haven’t. Most people don’t read it, and there are good reasons for that.

    First of all, the Terms & Conditions are 99% of the time longer than your history homework of an entire month. PayPal’s Terms & Conditions has far more words than the Terms & Conditions of other companies that offer similar services. To read PayPal's Terms & Conditions, the average reader would need 1.5 hours. That`s a lot of time for reading a boring document that`s written with many long sentences. Especially when the whole point of using services like PayPal is to make quick transactions.

    Isn’t there a law that protects us and our personal data to be used by PayPal?

    Luckily, we have the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). This is a long document that holds laws and rules around data privacy that all businesses collecting and processing personal data in the European Union (EU) have to follow. This document sets the bar very high, and many businesses that collect data had to make changes to improve their users’ data protection when it was introduced in 2018.

    Here is one article from the GDPR that sets rules about how personal data should be processed:


    GDPR Article 5

    „1. Personal data shall be:

    Adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purpose for which they are processed (data minimization).“

    Here comes the problem: It is not defined what ‘adequate, relevant and limited’ data is, so companies can - and, disclaimer, they do - interpret it any way they want to. Therefore, PayPal may collect any data as long as they can justify it with their business interests. PayPal’s business interests are not really obvious to the user and are described vaguely, so PayPal can justify collecting and processing basically anything. And this is what they do.

    "But what does this have to do with me?"

    Imagine you are out with a group of friends to grab some ice cream. One person in your group forgot their wallet and asked if someone could lend her some money. You look at her as she asks the question, and notice the bright yellow sweater she is wearing. You remember that a while ago, you lent money to another friend, which you never got back. “What a coincidence,” you think, “my friend has the exact same sweater.” Suddenly, you don't want to borrow that person in the yellow sweater money anymore…

    Let’s take this example to another level: Imagine the effect that such a decision would have when someone applies for a job. Just because the applicant had the same hairstyle or the same coat as a candidate that was not so good at the interview doesn’t tell if that person will behave the same way as another person with the same look. But if the applicant doesn’t get the job because the manager now sees the person in a worse light, the applicant is treated unfairly. Unfortunately, something like that happens all the time, with possibly serious effects for the individual.

    Luckily, it’s not allowed to make decisions based on someone’s race, gender, sexual orientation or religion. That’s a really good thing, but does that also apply for algorithms that make decisions for humans? Of course it does, but algorithms are better than humans in finding patterns and connections between characteristics of individuals. An algorithm might have never gotten information about protected characteristics, such as race, gender, sexual orientation or religion, but algorithms do an excellent job at finding characteristics that correlate with the protected class. For example, zip code could correlate with race; or shopping behavior with gender. This is called proxy discrimination.

    Now we’re talking about humans. But what is the case when algorithms make decisions instead of humans?

    Any variable that correlates with a protected variable can be used to make predictions. Even if the algorithm has no information about race, gender or religion, it uses proxies that say something about a protected group of people. This leads to treating people the same as people who have behaved similarly in the past. People are put into boxes based on their behavior and those boxes are used to make decisions.

    You might wonder “what’s the problem?”. Well, the more often we are using any kind of service or application, we generate more and more data that can be used to find so-called ‘hidden patterns’ in the data that correlate with a protected class. This makes it easier for the algorithm to correlate a protected class with other characteristics (and we don’t want that).

    Are we now finally at the point where I learn what this has to do with me?

    Yes, we are. You probably also know someone in your friend group or family who doesn’t use Instagram, TikTok or PayPal or shares very little because that person doesn’t want their data to be aggregated. That’s a fair decision, but is it really that easy to protect yourself? The more people use applications that collect large amounts of data, the more personal information about different people gets collected. Those large amounts of data can help to fill in missing pieces for people who decided not to share their personal data online. This is called Predictive Analytics.

    The algorithms has no chance of checking whether the personal information about you is actually correct, it just assumes it because it seems to be best fit based on that one piece of information. You are placed in a box with people that seem similar to you. The decisions of your friends and everyone else on the planet to share personal information have an effect on all the people who don’t share personal information. When sharing personal information online, we don’t only make a decision for ourselves, but also for all the people who actually don’t want to share their personal information. If enough people provide enough data, then predictions can be made about people who haven’t shared their own data. This is called “data aggregation”.

    Correlation

    A correlation describes the connection between two things.
    Many people buy ice cream on hot summer days. Because the sun is so intense, many people also get a sunburn. There is a correlation between the amount of sunburned people and the amount of ice cream that is eaten. If more people are eating ice cream, more people will also be sunburned. However, the correlation between two things says nothing about the cause. The amount of ice cream that is eating does not cause people to get a sunburn, or the other way around. In this case, the cause for the amount of sunburned people and the amount of ice cream eaten is a third variable, namely the hot summer weather.

    Algorithms and Artificial Intelligence (AI)

    Those words sound complicated and big, but now we want to take a short moment to break them down.

    Algorithms

    Artificial Intelligence (AI)






    Algorithms

    An algorithm is a specific set of rules that are applied step-by-step to get a solution to a problem. A simple example is a recipe for baking a cake. You have a step-by-step instruction about what you have to do to have a delicious cake in the end. (Just make sure next time you’re sharing a recipe with someone, not to say “I have an amazing cheesecake algorithm for you”, that’s probably going to confuse your fellow baker!)

    Artificial Intelligence (AI)

    AI systems hold such algorithms to solve very complex systems, such as recognizing objects on images. Because such algorithms are too hard to understand for humans, AI systems find the solutions and algorithms themselves. Imagine you have an image of a dog and want to determine which breed it is. Some smart person has created an AI for that by taking hundreds of thousands of dog images with the breed name and showing it to the AI. The AI learns which features are unique to a labrador, a pug or a dalmatian. You can then take any dog image you want, and the AI will tell you which breed it is. What makes AI systems ‘intelligent’ is that they are able to learn the algorithms by themselves and they can then determine the breed very well from dog images that the AI has never seen before.

    Nudging

    Do you think that your choices online are really yours?
    That you are the one that has made the decision to share your data?

    Nudging strategies use cognitive shortcuts in decision making to make irrational behavior more explainable and even predictable.

    Although you are ultimately free to decide, what you agreed to is most probably the result of companies’ efforts and psychological tricks. People’s behavior is predictable!
    This fact can be used to get desired outcomes or manipulate users. The specific placement of certain buttons can nudge you into a certain direction. This makes you think that the choice was yours, while still allowing the company to get their desired outcome.

    Dark Patterns

    Sometimes, a nudge can become a dark pattern if it’s designed to make you choose something with bad consequences for you.

    “To share” or “not to share”? That is the question!

    Well, in this case, it’s barely a question, since you’re getting pushed to “share” without even considering the alternative. That’s what dark patterns and nudging do.
